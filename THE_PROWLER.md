## College Sports Just Changed Forever

For decades, college athletes couldn't make a dime from their own name. A quarterback with half a million Instagram followers couldn't accept $200 from a local restaurant to post about them. If they did, the NCAA would strip their eligibility â€” they couldn't play.

That era is over.

**July 2021** â€” The NCAA changed the rules. Athletes can now profit from their **NIL** â€” their **N**ame, **I**mage, and **L**ikeness. Endorsement deals, social media sponsorships, paid appearances, merchandise â€” all fair game. A brand-new market was born overnight.

**June 2025** â€” A massive lawsuit called **House v. NCAA** was settled. This reshaped the entire landscape. Schools can now directly share up to **~$20 million per year** of their revenue with athletes. The old amateur model is officially dead.

**July 2025** â€” The **College Sports Commission (CSC)** launched â€” a new independent body with the authority to enforce NIL rules and declare athletes ineligible. They created an online reporting system called **NIL Go** where athletes must disclose their deals.

**The critical rule:** Any deal worth **$600 or more** must be reported through NIL Go within **5 business days.** Miss the deadline and the athlete risks losing their eligibility to play.

These are 18-22 year olds juggling school, practice, games â€” and now they're suddenly running what amounts to a small business. They're forgetting to file. They're losing eligibility over paperwork.

> **The NIL market is $2.75 billion as of 2025.** The average deal is about $1,500 â€” not million-dollar mega-contracts, but thousands of everyday deals between local businesses and athletes. And every single one that crosses $600 triggers mandatory reporting.
